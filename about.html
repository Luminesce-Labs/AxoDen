About

A compositional safety and evidence architecture for high-stakes AI

AxoDen is a compositional safety and evidence architecture designed for AI systems operating in high-stakes, regulated, or mission-critical environments.

AxoDen is a compositional safety and evidence architecture designed for AI systems operating in high-stakes, regulated, or mission-critical environments.

Its purpose is not to make optimistic claims about AI becoming universally safe or correct. Its purpose is to make safety, assurance, and governance more structural: bounding uncertainty, limiting unsupported model influence, engineering independence where it matters, and producing replayable evidence for audit, review, and certification-facing use.

AxoDen starts from a different premise than most AI tooling. Much of the industry focuses on output testing, monitoring, and policy overlays. AxoDen treats safety as a systems problem: how evidence is formed, how uncertainty is constrained, how decisions are gated, how degraded states are handled, and how reasoning can be replayed and inspected after the fact.

What I do

Five connected strata from theory to deployable assurance

My work spans five connected strata, from foundational theory to deployable assurance systems.

1. Foundational mathematics

I develop the mathematical foundations behind AxoDen’s safety model.

This includes work on Entropy-Bounded Data Pipelines (EBDP), Entropy-Frugal Intelligence (EFI), and Coprime-Factor Security (CFS) — a family of controls that treat uncertainty, unsupported model contribution, and component independence as quantities that can be constrained structurally rather than managed only through testing.

I also develop the Topology of Reasoning programme, which asks whether the shape of an evidence space affects what can be safely inferred or certified from it. In this view, reasoning is not only about content; it also has structure, and that structure can matter mathematically.

Alongside this, I work on extensions into quantum-classical safety composition and information-theoretic / order-theoretic foundations for certifiable machine reasoning.

2. Canonical formalisation

A large part of my work is turning theory into a coherent formal system.

That means building and maintaining the canonical layer that keeps formulas, semantics, evidence types, action states, and assurance claims consistent across papers, implementations, product architecture, and audit-facing materials.

This work matters because complex technical programmes often fail through semantic drift long before they fail mathematically. AxoDen is designed to remain coherent from theory through deployment.

3. AxoDen-Kernel

I develop the AxoDen-Kernel, the runtime assurance core of the system.

The kernel is designed as a deterministic safety and evidence control plane for high-stakes AI. It combines explicit evidence handling, replayable decision chains, fail-closed policy enforcement, runtime monitoring, degradation management, and topology-aware safety controls.

Core mechanisms include Adaptive Runtime Verification (ARV), the Monitoring Quad (MQ), explicit VSR state management, and certificate-based topological verification. The aim is to move safety away from informal heuristics and toward runtime structures that are inspectable, replayable, and contract-driven.

4. Assurance architecture

My work also extends into trust, attestation, and epistemic assurance.

This includes ASIL-M, which formalises inference-plane trust independence through multi-root assurance, thresholded independence metrics, and deterministic trust-gating artifacts. ASIL-M is also positioned in a standards-facing context through the IETF process, which matters for regulated and procurement-driven environments.

I also developed the Reflexive Epistemic Fabric, which applies structural analysis not only to external evidence but to the system’s own assumption and dependency fabric. The goal is not to prove that a machine is universally correct, but to determine whether its reasoning remains structurally coherent relative to its own assumptions and evidence contracts.

5. Domain systems and applied wedges

The AxoDen architecture is designed to operate in real domains, not only as theory.

Current applied directions include:

  • AxoDen Forensic Intelligence — evidence-disciplined triage and replayable investigative support for cyber and SOC settings
  • AxoDen Spectrum Bridge — deterministic RF-to-AI evidence mediation for defence and aerospace environments
  • AxoDen Micro-Grid Integrity — cyber-physical assurance concepts, including replay-grade control and Grid Flight Recorder architecture for critical infrastructure

These wedges matter because they show how the same underlying safety logic can move across software, sensing, and physical actuation environments.

Why it matters

A structural assurance path for systems where failure matters

Current baseline

Most AI safety work remains largely behavioural and statistical: test outputs, evaluate performance, sample failure, and hope the distribution holds.

Different path

I focus on structural assurance: designing systems so that uncertainty is bounded, unsupported inference is constrained, independence is engineered, degradation is explicit, and decisions are backed by replayable evidence.

Programme scope

That approach is closer to how safety is treated in regulated and mission-critical domains. It is also why AxoDen is broader than a single model, a single tool, or a single paper.
